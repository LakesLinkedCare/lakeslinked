AI Clinical Trials Registered in the Last 2 Months: The Field Is Moving From Accuracy to Outcomes

The next phase of clinical AI is not chatbots. It is population-scale evidence.

Sergey Soshnikov, MD, PhD Public-Health Physician-Scientist · Founder, Lakes Linked
Share LinkedIn Facebook
Abstract

Since 1 May 2026, 154 AI-related clinical trials have been registered on ClinicalTrials.gov. Read as a set rather than a list, they mark a shift in clinical AI — away from diagnostic-accuracy demonstrations and toward the questions public health turns on: survival, screening yield, antimicrobial stewardship, workflow integration, equity, and implementation at scale. Diagnostic imaging still dominates the count, but the studies that matter attach hard endpoints, embed models inside the electronic record, or benchmark general-purpose language models against clinicians under prospective conditions. This brief organises the set by function, scale, endpoint, and public-health relevance; spotlights five trials that show the direction of travel; and reads the signal for researchers, policymakers, and implementation scientists. Every NCT links to its source record.

01 · Method of readingHow to read the signal

A ClinicalTrials.gov search is only useful once it is read for meaning rather than counted. A raw query returns a list; the signal appears when trials are interpreted by function (what the model does at the point of care), by scale (who is running population-sized cohorts), by endpoints (accuracy metrics versus survival, length of stay, mortality), and by public-health relevance (whether the study touches screening, stewardship, equity, or implementation). A single trial can carry more than one of these signals.

Two facts frame everything that follows. The headline: diagnostic imaging still leads new registrations, yet a fast-growing minority test large language models head-to-head against clinicians, and a few push AI into population-scale opportunistic screening — the most public-health-relevant frontier. The caveat: most studies remain observational diagnostic-accuracy work, and only a handful carry hard clinical endpoints like 30-day mortality. Those are the ones worth watching.

03 · SpotlightsFive trials that show where the field is going

Chosen not for novelty alone but for direction of travel — population-scale screening, antimicrobial stewardship, hard endpoints, EHR implementation, clinician–AI benchmarking, and infectious-disease surveillance. These are the studies asking public-health questions, not demo questions.

NCT06528223 200,000 participantsRCTZhejiang University

PANDA — opportunistic pancreatic-cancer screening from ordinary CT

A deep-learning model re-reads routine non-contrast CT scans to flag pancreatic cancer that radiologists missed. Patients who are imaging-negative but PANDA-positive get recalled for gold-standard work-up; the primary endpoint is overall survival.

Why it matters: this is opportunistic screening at population scale — the single most consequential public-health use of clinical AI. Pancreatic cancer is almost always caught too late; re-reading scans people already have, then tying the model to an overall-survival endpoint rather than an AUC, is precisely the move from accuracy to outcomes. If it works, the screening pathway costs almost nothing to deploy because the imaging already exists.
NCT06163781 30-day mortality endpointRCT · 7,584Amsterdam UMC

ABC — machine learning to stop unnecessary blood cultures in the ED

A model predicts whether a blood culture will come back positive. Below 5% probability, the test is cancelled. Validation shows it can cut blood-culture volume by at least 30% — the trial tests non-inferiority on 30-day mortality, admissions and length of stay.

Why it matters: antimicrobial stewardship is a core public-health priority, and this is one of the very few AI trials willing to defend a hard safety endpoint — 30-day mortality — rather than diagnostic accuracy. By cancelling low-yield blood cultures it targets both antibiotic overuse and false-positive contamination, the everyday drivers of resistance. A non-inferiority result here is what turns a prediction model into a defensible change in practice.
NCT07604662 25,518 participantsCluster-RCTUCSF

EHR-embedded AKI risk score after surgery

A pragmatic three-arm cluster-randomised trial of an acute-kidney-injury risk score built into the electronic record — tested with and without a "best practice advisory" nudge to clinicians.

Why it matters: this is implementation science, not model-building. A risk score only helps if it is embedded in the EHR, surfaced in the workflow, and acted on — and the three-arm design (score alone, score plus advisory, usual care) isolates exactly which part of that chain changes outcomes. It is the question that decides whether the thousands of published prediction models ever earn their place inside a real health system.
NCT07626060 LLM head-to-head690 · ED chest painMarmara University

GPT-4o vs Claude vs three-expert consensus on the HEART score

A prospective validation pitting two frontier language models against a three-expert gold standard for HEART-score calculation and 30-day major-adverse-cardiac-event prediction in emergency chest-pain patients.

Why it matters: general-purpose LLMs are already in clinicians' pockets, so the relevant question is no longer whether they sound plausible but whether they hold up against a defined gold standard on a real triage decision with a 30-day outcome. Formal, prospective clinician–AI benchmarking — rather than leaderboard scores on exam questions — is how the field earns the right to put these tools near patients.
NCT07611695 31,600 participantsObservationalHuashan Hospital

AI-driven tuberculosis landscape analysis & stratification

A large-scale effort to map and risk-stratify pulmonary tuberculosis across a population using AI — squarely in the infectious-disease, public-health surveillance lane.

Why it matters: tuberculosis is still one of the world's leading infectious killers, and case-finding — not treatment — is the binding constraint. Risk-stratifying tens of thousands of people is AI used as surveillance infrastructure: it directs scarce screening and treatment capacity toward those most likely to be missed. This is the public-health lane where AI's marginal value is highest, precisely because human screening cannot scale to meet the need.

05 · InterpretationThe public health signal

Accuracy is no longer enough. The most credible new trials treat a high AUC as the starting point, not the result. Diagnostic imaging still leads, but the studies worth attention attach survival, length of stay, or mortality to the model — a quiet admission that performance on a test set says little about whether patients are better off.

Opportunistic screening is becoming population-scale. PANDA (200,000) and the routine-CT tumour cohort (100,000) point to AI's largest public-health lever: extracting new diagnoses from scans people already have, at a marginal cost close to zero. This is where AI stops being a clinic tool and starts behaving like screening infrastructure.

LLMs are entering formal clinical benchmarking. General-purpose models like GPT-4o and Claude are now being tested prospectively against clinician gold standards on defined decisions — a shift from exam-style leaderboards toward the evidentiary bar real triage requires. It is early, narrow, and exactly the right direction.

Hard outcomes remain rare — and therefore valuable. Most registrations still measure diagnostic accuracy, not patient outcomes. That scarcity is what makes the exceptions — ABC's 30-day mortality endpoint and the UCSF AKI cluster-RCT — disproportionately important: they are the studies capable of moving guidelines rather than dashboards.

The takeaway

For public health, the most important question is no longer whether an AI model can predict. The harder question is whether it improves decisions, reduces harm, reaches the right populations, and works safely inside real health systems.

At Lakes Linked, we track that shift — from AI performance claims to public-health-relevant evidence: scale, endpoints, implementation, and equity.
