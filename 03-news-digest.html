AI in clinical care: what is actually deployed, and what the numbers really mean

Cardiac arrest dispatch, maternal heart failure detection, radiology at 22 hospital sites, and the unsolved fragmentation problem in epidemic surveillance — with corrected sourcing and evidence grading for each claim.

Evidence disclosure. Sources in this digest include vendor websites, health system announcements, trade press (AHA market scan, Becker's), and one peer-reviewed perspective article. None of the operational claims below have been independently replicated or published in an RCT. Where a figure comes from a vendor or institution reporting on its own deployment, this is noted. Treat all figures as preliminary and institution-reported unless otherwise stated. Click any source link to read the original.

01 Lead story

Emergency response · 911 dispatch · vendor-reported

AI on emergency calls: a reported 13% reduction in stroke handling time at Seattle Medic One

Corti — a Danish AI platform originally built to detect cardiac arrest patterns during emergency calls — is deployed at Seattle Fire Department's Medic One, which handles 149,000 calls per year. According to Corti's own case study data, stroke call handling time fell by approximately 13% following deployment; Boston EMS and SOS Alarm (Sweden) report similar operational improvements. Note on evidence: these figures are vendor-reported, not from an independent peer-reviewed study. Corti's strongest published evidence base is for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest detection (Blomberg et al., Resuscitation 2021), where prospective data exists. The stroke application is newer and less studied. Whether results at a large urban EMS system like Medic One would generalize to rural or under-resourced settings is an open question.

02 Clinical deployments: what health systems are reporting

Four cases from AHA's May 2026 health system scan and Becker's June 2026 roundup. These are institution-reported outcomes, not RCT findings — each is labeled accordingly.

Maternal health · Mayo Clinic · institution-reported

AI stethoscope associated with doubled detection of peripartum cardiomyopathy

Mayo Clinic deployed an AI-powered digital stethoscope to screen for peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) — a serious but rare heart condition affecting approximately 1 in 2,000 pregnant people. PPCM is a significant contributor to maternal cardiac mortality in the US (cardiovascular disease as a category is among the leading causes of pregnancy-related death, per CDC MMWR). According to Mayo Clinic's self-reported data, physicians identified twice as many patients with PPCM compared to standard diagnostic pathways — though the baseline rate and comparison period are not specified in the available reporting. Spontaneous recovery occurs in roughly 50–70% of PPCM patients; earlier detection expands the treatment window and avoids delayed diagnosis in the remainder.

Fetal imaging · Mount Sinai West · institution-reported

FDA-cleared AI ultrasound: 97%+ detection rate for major congenital heart defects

Mount Sinai West (New York City) reports using an FDA-cleared AI ultrasound tool to screen for major structural congenital heart defects (CHD) during routine prenatal care, achieving a detection rate above 97%. Major structural CHD affects roughly 6–8 per 1,000 births. The 97% figure is institution-reported; the comparison arm, reference standard, and whether this reflects sensitivity, PPV, or overall accuracy are not specified in the AHA market scan. The meaningful clinical benefit of tools like this is largest in settings where specialist echocardiographers are not available — primary care and rural obstetric settings — where the equity argument is strongest.

Radiology · Advocate Health · projected

AI imaging at 22 sites: Advocate Health projects 63,000 patients per year reached

Advocate Health (Wisconsin and North Carolina) embedded AI imaging models into radiology workflows at 22 sites beginning October 2024. The models flag time-critical findings: pulmonary embolisms, intracranial hemorrhages, rib and cervical spine fractures, pneumothorax, aortic dissections, and brain aneurysms. Advocate projects the deployment will reach nearly 63,000 patients per year through accelerated diagnosis and faster specialist notification. This figure is a forward projection from the institution, not an observed outcome — follow-up data on actual patient impact has not been published.

Physician documentation · self-reported survey data

Ambient AI scribes: clinicians report less fatigue, but survey methodology matters

Sanford Health reports that after less than one year with ambient AI documentation tools, 88% of clinicians said they felt less burnout and 95% said it reduced mental strain — from internal surveys. AdventHealth (Nuance DAX Copilot, ~2,000 physicians): 86% reported less burnout, 80% better patient experience. These figures are self-reported on employer-administered surveys and are subject to novelty effects and social desirability bias; they do not use validated burnout instruments (e.g., Maslach). They are meaningful as early signals of clinician experience, not as clinical outcome data. Separately: nearly two-thirds of Epic-connected hospitals have deployed some form of ambient AI as of January 2026 — a measure of spread, not of impact.

03 Epidemic surveillance: the fragmentation problem

Public health · epidemic intelligence · perspective article, Frontiers in AI 2025

BlueDot flagged early COVID signals before WHO's first statement. The integration gap that followed remains open.

On December 31, 2019, BlueDot's commercial AI platform — which scans news, airline data, and public health feeds in dozens of languages — sent client alerts flagging an unusual pneumonia cluster in Wuhan and assessed it as a potential international spread risk. WHO issued its first public notice the same day. The distinction is meaningful: BlueDot's system surfaced the signal from unstructured public data before official confirmation, demonstrating the potential of AI-driven open-source intelligence for outbreak early warning. EPIWATCH (UNSW) and HealthMap (launched 2006) operate on similar principles.

A 2025 perspective paper in Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence (Kaur & Butt, University of Waterloo / National Research Council Canada) argues that a structural problem persists: existing systems operate in silos. A hospital admission surge, a social media symptom spike, and a WHO notification may all point to the same outbreak — but arrive in separate platforms, analyzed separately, never synthesized into a unified signal. The authors propose a new LLM-based architecture (PandemicLLM) to integrate genomic, policy, and behavioral streams into a single real-time framework. Note: this is a perspective article, not a research study, and PandemicLLM is the authors' own proposed solution — independent validation does not yet exist. Key structural barriers beyond GDPR/HIPAA include the absence of standardized disease ontologies across systems and persistent underfunding of subnational surveillance infrastructure.
